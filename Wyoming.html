Yellowstone National Park

The world's first national park was founded in 1872. Yellowstone National Park sparked my interest in geology, and continues to leave me awestruck with each visit. A large portion of the park contains a caldera that is the result of three massive volcanic eruptions which occurred millions of years ago. The park sits atop a thin layer of Earth's crust. Its proximity to partial molten magma is responsible for many interesting geothermal features, including hot springs, geysers, mudpots, and fumaroles.

In addition to geothermal features, Yellowstone also features mountains, valleys, lakes, forests, and waterfalls. One of my favorite places in the park is the Grand Canyon of the Yellowstone, pictured above. Carved by the Yellowstone River, this canyon doesn't compare to the Grand Canyon in size but the colors are astounding!

Grand Teton National Park

Located just south of Yellowstone, Grand Teton National Park is named after the highest peak in the Teton Range, a prominent and picturesque range within the Rocky Mountains. The park is an escape into serenity. There are many miles of hiking trails around lakes and through rugged mountain wilderness.
